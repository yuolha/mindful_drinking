Sparkling Wine

In 1718, the memoirs of Abbot Godino, the canon of the Reims Cathedral, were published. The Abbot indicated that wines with a light color, almost white, saturated with gas, have appeared in champagne since about 1668. This led to a solid addiction to sparkling wines in France.

The inability to explain the unusual properties of sparkling wines gave rise to various assumptions. Some believed that the sparkles were associated with some additives, others thought that wines were made from immature grapes, and others saw this as a result of the Moon’s effect on bottling wine into bottles.

Dom Pérignon, a Benedictine monk, who showed exceptional abilities as a winemaker, was invited in 1670 to be in charge of the wine cellars of Abbey d’Hautvillers.

He is credited with obtaining white wines from red grapes intended for the preparation of sparkling wines and ways to care for them. Dom Pérignon, a great taster, for the first time in the history of winemaking, understood the importance of wine blends in champagne, knowing how different shades of taste were associated with the place where grapes grew.

All sparkling wines, including champagne, are about communication in all its forms. Champagne is a great communicator, able to reconcile the irreconcilable.

Champagne, if you are seeking the truth, is better than a lie detector. It encourages a man to be expansive, even reckless, while lie detectors are only a challenge to tell lies successfully. (Graham Greene)

The three of the world’s most famous champagnes unveil the art of communication as follows:

• Moët & Chandon Imperial gives us an understanding, that even an unworthy person deserves a chance in life and communication.
• Dom Pérignon represents someone who knows and understands many things, a person of a great mind who also knows the right moment to express their thoughts and opinions. For this reason, Dom Pérignon is very different every year.
• Veuve Clicquot is a deliberate provocation in communication; it is a challenge. Just like the young widow of Francois Clicquot accepted her challenge in managing the winery after her husband’s unexpected death, this champagne is full of vigor and determination.

There’s one more type of sparkling wine I want to talk about. Prosecco symbolizes the Italian dolce vita, “sweet life.” It is a celebration of the simple joys of life. The trick is to acknowledge these joys and share them with your loved ones.

If I were to compare champagne with a book or a movie character, I would say it is The Great Gatsby.
