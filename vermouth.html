Vermouth

Vermouth was explicitly created for women in the 18th century and is still more of a female drink today. Vermouth was a drink of relaxation for a woman of the patriarchal world. But some say that it is fairer to associate Hyppocrate with the first “vermouth” in human history. Mixing wine with herbs and spices is quite an ancient practice.

There is a film from the 90s that accurately reflects the essence of vermouth — "The First Wives Club.” College girlfriends met many years later at the funeral of another friend, who committed suicide because her husband left her for a younger woman. Reunited at the funeral, women understand that they have invested a lot of energy in the success of their men, but at the same time, they also remain unnecessary and discarded. They decide to take revenge, but in the end, revenge leads them to something they never expected — self-understanding. With the money "raised" from their ex-husbands, they open a crisis center for women.

Vermouth helps smooth out emotional states that may occur due to hormonal imbalances in a woman’s body, but it does not solve the problem. Nevertheless, this is the perfect drink for a bachelorette party: “Just relax, and let go.”

Suffering is as necessary to entertaining as vermouth is to a Martini - a small but vital ingredient. (Phyllis McGinley)

I won’t thoroughly analyze the drink’s composition — there are too many herbs and spices, but the word “vermouth” itself means “wormwood.” I mentioned it in the context of absinthe (wormwood). Vermouth uses alpine wormwood (Artemísia umbellifórmis), which is less dramatic than Artemisia absinthium. At the same time, it carries the central theme of the entire Wormwood family — a sense of threat and abuse, a desire for autonomy. Other essential components of vermouth are:

Sage (Salvia officinalis) represents someone who gives too much regardless of what comes in return. This person wants to live a life of joy and meaning but may burn out when they discover nothing left. Everything’s given away. Being so prone to sacrifice, a person of sage should carefully mind the balance of giving and receiving. Besides, there’s so much talent to offer, so many artistic skills, and inner wisdom.

Cinnamon (Cinnamomum verum) represents a person afraid to interact with the world independently. Like a child, the cinnamon person wants to hide away in a safe, familiar place full of care and understanding. There is a contradiction between the desire for communication and the inability to do so. Words are different from actions.

I also want to mention the role of olive, which has become a companion of vermouth.

European olive (Olea europea) expresses the conflict between love and family duty. This is an urgent need for love. Love relationships (outside the family) that cannot be realized. Children come first. They are caring about creating a legacy for future generations.

The modern world looks different compared to the reality of 18th-century vermouth drinkers. Women are used to executing their rights and freedoms. Men show more respect and support in the undertakings of their sisters, wives, and daughters. A lot has changed. Still, vermouth is a popular drink amongst both men and women. It relieves the extreme pressure of expectations and the unjustified and pernicious speed at which our society lives. Too much stress, too much of everything, too little time to take a deep breath and reconsider, define what matters. So vermouth is like a retreat for the lonely, tired, and confused. Even if it’s the cool and adventurous James Bond with a Vesper Martini in his hand. By the way, Vesper Martini includes gin, vodka, dry vermouth, and a lemon peel. You already know these ingredients’ essence, so you can analyze almost any cocktail.
