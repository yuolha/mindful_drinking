Beer

Beer has a very ancient history. Perhaps you’ll be surprised to know that some traces of beermaking were found in caves of Israel 13 thousand years ago. In the 7th century B.C., Sumerians manufactured beer on a large scale. Ancient Egyptians and Greeks also brew beer. Egyptians made it mandatory for their warriors and builders of the pyramids, which is very interesting concerning the essence of the drink. Celts and Germans, and many other European tribes brew beer, which is why Romans regarded beer to be a barbarian drink.

In Europe, Germany made considerable input into the development of the drink. Since the 8th century A.D., German monasteries played a central part in brewing, and by the 14th century, it became an extremely profitable business. The people of the British Isles knew beer long before the Norman invasion. 

I see beer as an exclusively medicinal drink, not a recreational one. I prefer ale, and there is a reason, which will become clear as we go further in our analysis of the drink. The impact of beer on the human system depends on its fermentation type.
